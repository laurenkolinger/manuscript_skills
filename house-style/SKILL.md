# House Style

These are the writing mechanics for every piece of prose, regardless of audience or format. They govern how the writing reads, not what argument it makes. Apply them to emails, summaries, proposals, slide text, analyses, reports, captions, and any finished deliverable. The aim is writing that sounds like an accountable person did real work and stated it plainly.

This layer is strategy-free on purpose. Choices about framing, emphasis, and persuasion belong to the specific task. Here we only fix the prose.

## The rules

### 1. No em dashes
Never use em dashes. Write grammatically complete sentences using periods, commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses instead. When tempted to splice with a dash, split into two sentences or set the aside off with commas or parentheses.

Avoid: The team mapped the reef — a first for the site — using the AUV.
Use: The team mapped the reef using the AUV, a first for the site.

### 2. State the claim affirmatively
Lead with the positive claim. Defensive structures like "not X, but Y" make the reader carry a negation and signal that you expect doubt. Say what is true and let the contrast stay implicit.

Avoid: This is not just monitoring.
Use: This work turns monitoring into spatially explicit reef intelligence that guides action.

Avoid: Rather than a fish project, this validates AUV behavior.
Use: The aggregation provides a field setting where the team validates adaptive AUV behavior.

### 3. Match tense to commitment
Tense tells the reader what is finished, what exists now, and what is promised. Keep the three separate so a reader can see exactly where the work stands.

- Past tense for completed work: "The team deployed the AUV at Flat Cay and recorded 246 observations."
- Present tense for current capacity: "VICAR operates the GPU workstations that process the imagery."
- Future tense for planned work: "The team will train models to flag bleaching and disease."
- Future-oriented language for vision: "The long-term goal is a resident network of survey AUVs."

Describing planned work in the present, or vision as if already built, reads as overclaiming. Hold the line between them.

### 4. Put a real actor in the subject
Name who does the work. Agentless process sentences ("the workflow generates maps") hide responsibility and read as evasive. Let people, teams, and named partners act. Let tools and workflows be the things those actors use.

Avoid: Field missions collect synchronized imagery and the platform produces maps.
Use: The team collects synchronized imagery during field missions and uses the workflow to produce maps.

Preferred subjects: we, the team, VICAR, CMES, named partners, the graduate student, field teams, managers.

### 5. Deliver the finished artifact, not the scaffolding
The reader should see the argument, not the process that built it. Strip planning talk, strategy notes, and meta-commentary. Convert private instructions into polished content rather than restating them.

Remove residue such as: "the user wants," "this should be framed as," "the goal here is to," "this section is meant to show," "we need to make clear that."

Private instruction: The fish work is really a testbed for reef tools.
Final content: Grammanik Bank gives the team a data-rich setting to validate adaptive AUV behavior before applying it to reef-conservation targets.

### 6. Write sentences that stand alone
Each sentence and each bullet should carry enough to be understood on its own. Fragment piles force the reader to reconstruct your meaning.

Avoid: AUVs. AI tools. Reef maps. Future network.
Use: The AUV collects imagery that AI models turn into reef maps, building toward a resident survey network.

### 7. Attribute to the working unit, not the umbrella
Prefer the specific group that does the work: the team, VICAR, CMES, named partners, or you. Reserve the parent-institution name for administrative cases where it is unavoidable, and use it sparingly.

## Quick pre-send check
Before returning any prose, confirm:
- No em dashes anywhere.
- Claims are stated affirmatively, with no "not X, but Y" left in.
- Past, present, and future tense match what is done, what exists, and what is planned.
- Each working sentence names who acts.
- No planning language, strategy notes, or "the user wants" residue remains.
- Bullets and sentences stand on their own.
- Attribution favors the working unit over the umbrella institution.

## Tense

Methods and Results describe completed work, so write them in the past tense (the
model was fitted; the bootstrap resampled sites; the 2005 bleaching caused the
largest decline). Reserve the present tense for what a figure or table shows and
for standing definitions, and the future tense for planned work not yet done.
This applies to the analysis reports as much as to the manuscript: write what each
step did, not what the script "does" as it runs.
