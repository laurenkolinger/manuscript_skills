# Scientific Results Writing for Data Summaries

## The three-skill scientific-writing suite (this is Skill 1)

This skill is the first of three that revise quantitative results prose. Apply them in
order, and diagnose the main failure mode before revising a paragraph.

1. `scientific-results-writing` (this skill) controls TONE. It removes metaphor,
   personification, promotion, and theatrical language so the prose reads as a Results
   section. Apply first.
2. `de-densify-scientific-prose` controls INFORMATION LOAD. It unpacks compressed
   sentences so the metric, denominator, unit, sample set, comparison, and
   interpretation are explicit, while keeping every number. Apply second.
3. `scientific-sentence-framing` controls SENTENCE ORDER. It puts a concrete subject
   first and keeps the number next to what it measures. Apply third.

Diagnose first: if the text sounds melodramatic, metaphorical, promotional, or
anthropomorphic, apply this skill (Skill 1); if it has the right information but feels
too compressed, apply Skill 2; if it forces the reader to enter sentences through
abstract phrases, qualifiers, or method terms, apply Skill 3. Most dense results
paragraphs need Skills 2 and 3 together, and some also need Skill 1.

Three rules hold across all three skills:

- **Preserve the scientific content.** Do not drop numbers, caveats, figure or table
  references, file names, uncertainty estimates, or sample sizes unless explicitly
  asked. If prose is too dense, split the sentence; do not delete information.
- **Make the text readable on first pass.** The reader should not have to infer what
  was measured, averaged, or summed, what the denominator or unit was, whether values
  are rates, totals, shares, or raw changes, whether change is signed or absolute, or
  which reefs, sites, events, or windows were included. Add missing nouns, define the
  frame, split long sentences.
- **Avoid possessives.** Use "total change for each reef" rather than "reef's total
  change", "share for each event" rather than "event's share", "bars in Figure 2"
  rather than "Figure 2's bars".

The suite runs as a main revision step at the beginning, middle, and end of a writing
task, and as a hard gate in the review and completion-audit steps.

## Purpose

Use this skill when writing or revising scientific results, figure captions, table notes, inspection notes, or narrative summaries that describe quantitative data.

The goal is to produce clear, professional scientific prose that sounds like a real manuscript, technical report, grant result, or ecological monitoring writeup. The writing should be direct, measured, and data-centered. It should not sound cinematic, metaphorical, promotional, or like a popular-science article.

## Core Principle

Let the data carry the force of the statement.

Do not dramatize results with figurative language. If a result is large, important, unusual, or severe, show that through the numbers, comparisons, uncertainty, and sampling coverage.

## Required Style

Write in plain scientific prose.

Use precise statistical language:
- median
- mean
- interquartile range
- range
- percentage points
- site-level change
- reef-level change
- among-site variability
- number of reefs
- maximum observed loss
- sufficient data
- spatial coverage
- temporal coverage

Use complete sentences.

Prefer direct verbs:
- produced
- showed
- declined
- increased
- differed
- ranged
- was associated with
- affected
- exceeded
- accounted for
- was larger than
- was smaller than

Keep interpretation close to the data. Each sentence should either report a result, compare results, explain the statistic being used, or clarify how the table or figure should be read.

## Avoid This Language

Do not use metaphorical, cinematic, emotional, or personified language to describe data.

Avoid phrases like:
- took the most coral cover
- deeper bite
- cut as deep
- dwarfed
- the story tells us
- the data reveal a dramatic story
- reefs were hit hardest
- the event hammered the reefs
- the losses were devastating
- the reader meets the data
- spread the reefs apart
- left scars across the reefscape
- unfolded across the record
- the event carved through the reef community

Also avoid vague emphasis words unless they are immediately supported by data:
- dramatic
- massive
- huge
- major
- severe
- striking
- remarkable
- unprecedented

If one of these words is necessary, pair it with the exact statistic that justifies it.

## Preferred Framing

Use this structure for disturbance summaries:

1. State the main result directly.
2. Give the sample size or coverage.
3. Give the central tendency.
4. Give the spread or uncertainty.
5. Compare to other events using the same metric.
6. Explain what the metric represents, if needed.

Example structure:

"The 2005 bleaching event produced the largest coral-cover losses among the major disturbances. Across 26 reefs with sufficient data, the median site-level change was -5.9 percentage points (IQR: -10.7 to -2.1 pp). The 2010 disease event affected the same number of reefs and produced a smaller median loss of -2.6 pp (IQR: -5.5 to -0.5 pp)."

## Percentage Point Rule

Use "percentage points" or "pp" when describing absolute changes in percent cover.

Correct:
"Coral cover declined by 5.9 percentage points."

Incorrect:
"Coral cover declined by 5.9 percent."

Use percent only for relative change, and make that explicit.

Correct:
"Mean cover declined from 55.7% to 5.3%, a loss of 50.4 percentage points."

## Comparisons

When comparing events, use the same statistic across all events unless clearly stated otherwise.

Good:
"By median change, the 2005 bleaching loss was approximately 2.3 times larger than the 2010 disease loss."

Good:
"By mean change, the 2005 bleaching loss was approximately 1.7 times larger."

Avoid mixing medians, means, and extremes in the same comparison without explaining the shift.

## Site-Level Tables

When describing per-site tables, explain what each row and column represents without over-narrating.

Preferred wording:

"The per-site table summarizes reef-level trajectories across the full record. For each reef, it reports mean coral cover in the first and last survey years, net change across the record, median cover change during each well-powered disturbance, background change between disturbances, and the disturbance associated with the largest site-level loss."

Use "blank cells indicate missing or insufficient data" rather than more elaborate phrasing.

## Rare Events

For rare or low-coverage events, state why they are handled separately.

Preferred wording:

"Events recorded at only two to four reefs are shown in the figures rather than included in the main per-site table."

Do not imply that rare events are unimportant unless the data support that interpretation. Distinguish low sample size from low impact.

## Tone

The tone should be:
- clear
- concise
- technical
- calm
- precise
- manuscript-ready

The tone should not be:
- lyrical
- cinematic
- punchy
- promotional
- anthropomorphic
- emotionally heightened
- overly "story-driven"

## Revision Checklist

Before finalizing, check every sentence:

1. Does this sentence report, compare, or explain data?
2. Are the statistics named clearly?
3. Are percentage-point changes labeled correctly?
4. Are medians, means, IQRs, ranges, and sample sizes clearly distinguished?
5. Are comparisons made using consistent metrics?
6. Is any metaphor or personification present?
7. Could this sentence appear in a real Results section?

If the sentence sounds clever, rewrite it.

## Example: Bad to Good

Bad:
"The 2005 bleaching took the most coral cover from the typical reef and spread the reefs farthest apart while doing it."

Good:
"The 2005 bleaching event produced the largest median coral-cover loss and the greatest among-site variability among the major disturbances."

Bad:
"The 2005 bleaching cut about 2.3 times as deep as the disease."

Good:
"By median site-level change, the 2005 bleaching loss was approximately 2.3 times larger than the 2010 disease loss."

Bad:
"The spread tells the same story as the depth."

Good:
"The IQR showed the same pattern as the median loss, with greater among-site variability in 2005 than in the other major disturbances."

## Default Output Standard

When revising text under this skill, produce polished prose that could be pasted directly into a scientific manuscript, technical report, monitoring summary, grant report, or results narrative.

Do not add unnecessary explanation before or after the revised text unless requested.

## Scope and precedence

This skill governs the prose voice of every results narrative, figure caption,
figure title and subtitle, table caption, and data summary. It runs as the final
prose pass at every report step and as a gate in the review and completion-audit
steps. Where it conflicts with the cinematic or "story-first" tendencies of a
legibility pass, this skill wins for results prose: dense inline statistics stay,
metaphor and personification go.
