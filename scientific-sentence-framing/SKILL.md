# Sentence Framing for Scientific Results

## The three-skill scientific-writing suite

This skill is the third of three that revise quantitative results prose. Apply them in
order:

1. `scientific-results-writing` controls tone (remove metaphor and theater).
2. `de-densify-scientific-prose` controls information load (unpack compressed
   sentences, state the frame and denominator).
3. `scientific-sentence-framing` (this skill) controls the order of information within
   a sentence (concrete subject first, number next to what it measures).

Most dense results paragraphs need Skills 2 and 3 together, and some also need Skill 1.

## Purpose

Use this skill when scientific text contains the right information but the sentence
order makes the reader work too hard. The goal is to make each sentence easy to enter,
easy to follow, and easy to connect to the next. It is especially useful for results
text, figure descriptions, statistical summaries, and methods explanations.

## Core Problem

Many dense scientific sentences start with an abstract frame, method phrase, or
qualifier before naming the thing being described, which delays comprehension. Avoid
openings such as "On a per-year footing", "On raw totals", "With no rate
normalization", "Each bar", "This weighting", "The attribution", "The contrast", "The
apportionment", "In terms of contribution", and "For the purposes of comparison". These
force the reader to hold an abstract phrase in memory before learning what the sentence
is about.

## Main Rule

Start the sentence with the thing the reader needs to understand first. Strong sentence
subjects include coral cover, disturbance periods, periods between disturbances, the
2005 bleaching event, the disease outbreak, Figure 2, Figure 6, the rate comparison,
the raw-total comparison, the attribution analysis, the all-reef average, and the
among-impacted average.

## Subject Choice

Choose the sentence subject based on the job of the sentence.

Use the biological variable as the subject when reporting biological change.
Good: "Coral cover declined faster during disturbance periods than between
disturbances."
Avoid: "On a per-year footing, coral cover declined faster during disturbance windows
than between events."

Use the event as the subject when reporting event contribution.
Good: "The 2005 bleaching event accounted for 41% of total disturbance-driven
coral-cover change."
Avoid: "The largest single share of the coral-cover decline was the 2005 bleaching."

Use the figure as the subject when explaining what a figure shows.
Good: "Figure 6 compares raw coral-cover change during disturbance periods and periods
between disturbances."

Use the method as the subject only when the sentence is actually about the method.
Good: "The attribution analysis divided total disturbance-driven coral-cover change
among the recorded events."

## Sentence Order

Use this order inside a sentence whenever possible: subject, action, metric, group or
denominator, result.
"Coral cover declined by 1.21 percentage points per year during disturbance periods at
the typical reef."
or
"At the typical reef, coral cover declined by 1.21 percentage points per year during
disturbance periods."
Both are better than "On a per-year footing, the typical reef lost 1.21 percentage
points of cover per year during disturbance."

## Put Familiar Information Before New Information

Begin with something the reader already knows, then add the new result.
"Coral cover declined faster during disturbance periods than between disturbances. The
rate comparison used annualized coral-cover change, reported as percentage points per
year." The first sentence gives the main result; the second defines the metric. Avoid
starting with the metric before the reader knows why it matters.

## Avoid Front-Loaded Modifiers

Do not begin sentences with long qualifying phrases unless they are necessary for
contrast.
Avoid: "On raw totals, with no rate normalization, the disturbance windows accounted
for nearly all the net decline."
Better: "Figure 6 shows raw coral-cover change rather than annualized rates. In this
comparison, disturbance periods accounted for nearly all net coral-cover decline."

## Put the Comparison Close to the Number

Do not separate the value from the comparison group.
Good: "At the typical reef, coral cover declined by 1.21 pp per year during disturbance
periods, compared with 0.02 pp per year between disturbances."

## Use Repetition Strategically

Repeat key nouns when clarity matters.
Good: "The 2023 bleaching event contributed 11%. The 2024 bleaching event contributed
10%. The 2017 storm contributed 8%."
Avoid compressing too much: "The 2023 and 2024 bleaching (11 and 10 percent) and the
2017 storm (8 percent) divided most of the remainder." Repetition is better than
confusion.

## Avoid Possessives

Do not use possessive sentence frames.
Avoid: "Each bar is an event's share."
Use: "Each bar shows the share for one event."
Avoid: "That weighting made a reef's shares partition its loss."
Use: "This weighting made the event shares for each reef sum to 1."

## Use Figure Sentences as Wayfinding

A figure sentence should orient the reader before giving numbers.
"Figure 2 shows the share of total disturbance-driven coral-cover change attributed to
each event." Then give the numbers: "The 2005 bleaching event contributed 41%, followed
by the disease outbreak at 28%."

## Keep the Sentence Friendly to First Read

A sentence is probably too hard if it contains more than two of these at once: a figure
reference, a method term, a number, a denominator, a comparison, a caveat, a file
reference, a parenthetical, a ratio, a unit change. When that happens, split the
sentence.

## Final Sentence Test

For each sentence, ask:

1. What is the subject?
2. Is the subject concrete enough?
3. Does the subject match the purpose of the sentence?
4. Does the sentence begin with a frame the reader already understands?
5. Is the number close to the thing being measured?
6. Is the denominator close to the percentage or average?
7. Would the sentence still make sense if read alone?

## Default Output Standard

Produce revised prose that can be pasted directly into a manuscript, report, figure
caption, table note, grant report, or inspection note. The writing should be clear,
precise, calm, technical, readable on first pass, and free of front-loaded modifiers
and possessives. Do not add a long explanation before or after the revised text unless
requested.
